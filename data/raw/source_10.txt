I don't know what role the entry level position is... But here is some thoughts to help you start brainstorming ideas.

You have discovered an program that is doing unusual things. How do you triage it?

You read an recent article about a cyber attack and it mentioned a product you have seen/heard of in use on the network you looking at. What would you do?

You notice a gap in logging collection, how to you resolve this issue?

I hope these might stir the thought process some. No clue how close these would even be to what you will be asked... But could be a good exercise to try them out.

Best of luck, be you and if you don't know... It's not the end of the world. How you handle the situation of not knowing something is just as important as giving most perfect answer.

Best practice for tech interviewers is to use one of the things the candidate tells you they did and dig into it. Red flag if they are highlighting an achievement or skill and when you dig into it there's nothing of substance there. You say you're a network security guru but are not even somewhat familiar with common ports/protocols or RFC 1918 IP space?

Some open ended scenario prompts I have used to explore the candidate's skills and mindsets:

    a vulnerability scanner discovers a cross site scripting vulnerability associated with a dependency of a wordpress server that is scheduled to deploy in production in the next 48 hours. What would you need to know to assess severity/risk level of this finding and what actions would you take?

    suppose we used a vendor to archive data in cold storage. every year we send them an archive of the data we're required by law to retain for 7 years. This vendor contacts us to report that a breach has occurred and our data may have been accessed but they aren't sure yet. What would you need to know to make an assessment of whether our data was accessed? What would you need to know to assess what the damage to our organization might be?

The only real way to fail these scenario questions is to make assumptions without clarifying presuppositions which demonstrates your grasp on security concepts. The interviewer can inject new information to steer the conversation to explore where the candidate claims to have experience. If the candidate is very experienced, the interviewer can introduce ambiguity into the scenario and evaluate how the candidate makes decisions with imperfect information.


When I perform interviews I specifically include questions I don't expect people to know the answer to. It's perfectly normal not to know everything, and it's an important skill to be able to communicate when you don't.

It's also important to be familiar enough with industry topics to be able to form a plan on how you would gain the information.

"I don't know but I would probably look up guidance based on the SANS incident response framework. If I had to guess without looking, I'd probably do XYZ" is excellent for example
